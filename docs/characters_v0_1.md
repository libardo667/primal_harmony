# Pokémon Emerald: Primal Harmony
## Character Document — v0.1
### Player Character, The Holdfast Family, Faction Leaders, Key NPCs

---

## Design Philosophy

Characters in Primal Harmony are defined less by what they want and more by *how they carry what they've already been through*. Hoenn has been in slow crisis for over a decade by the time the main game begins. The people the player meets are not unscarred. They are not naive. They have developed the particular quality of people who have chosen, repeatedly, to keep going anyway — and that choice has shaped them in ways both beautiful and complicated.

Every major character should feel like they have a life that continues when the player is not looking at them.

---

## The Player Character

**Designation:** Pokémon Rehabilitator, Hoenn Ecological Recovery Initiative (provisional field certification)
**Age at game start (Phase Three):** 20
**Home base:** The Holdfast, Route 117
**Lineage:** Grandchild of The Holdfast's founders; raised partly at the waystation

### Character Philosophy

The player character is not a blank slate, but they are a vessel — defined enough to feel real, open enough to be inhabited. They have a history that informs their skills and their worldview, but their choices during Phase Three are genuinely theirs.

They grew up catching frightened Pokémon gently. They grew up building levees. They have a specific, embodied knowledge of what it means to care for something that cannot tell you what it needs. This is not a power fantasy origin. It is a *competence* origin — the player character is quietly, practically good at something that turns out to matter enormously.

### Player Choice — Gender and Name

Both player characters — one presenting as male, one as female — are grandchildren of the same couple. They are different people with the same history. The game uses gender-neutral third-person framing in all documents and environmental text where possible. NPCs use the player's chosen name consistently.

**Default names (placeholder):** River (male presenting), Sable (female presenting)

### Emotional Arc

**Phase One:** The player is absorbing. Watching. Learning. The world is large and mostly safe and the adults know what to do.

**Phase Two:** The player is contributing. The work is real and the stakes are real but the player's role is still supportive — they are becoming capable, not yet responsible.

**Phase Three:** The player is leading. The adults are still there, but the player is now the one who makes choices that matter. The emotional journey is the transition from *helping* to *deciding* — and all the weight that comes with that.

## The Holdfast Family

### The Grandmother

**Role:** Co-founder of The Holdfast; ecological recordkeeper; the player's deepest source of narrative context
**Age:** Late 60s
**Emotional register:** Warm, precise, carries grief without being defined by it

She started The Holdfast as a younger woman with her husband when Pokémon rehabilitation was considered a niche concern — something that happened when a Pokémon got injured on a route, not a response to systemic ecological collapse. Over decades she has watched her institution become more necessary than she ever wanted it to be.

She is the keeper of the Rehabilitation Log. She has named every Pokémon that has passed through since the beginning, even the ones that stayed only a day. She has a memory for faces — human and Pokémon alike — that the family has long attributed to some kind of gift. The truth is she has simply been paying attention for fifty years.

She tells the player, in Phase Two, about the Log. This is the moment the player inherits the philosophy of the whole game.

**Key line:** *"Your grandfather started this when we opened. He said someday it would matter to have the record. I think someday is now."*

**Narrative function:** She is the moral compass of Phase One and Phase Two. In Phase Three, she recedes to a support role — the player calls in, she knows what questions to ask, she never gives the answer directly. She trusts the player to find it.

### The Grandfather

**Role:** Co-founder of The Holdfast; the player's first field teacher; emotional anchor
**Age:** Early 70s
**Emotional register:** Unhurried, self-contained, occasionally devastating

He is the one who turned off the TV when Pacifidlog was reported. He is the one who, in the same motion, suggested they go check on the Pokémon in residence. This is not avoidance — it is the specific wisdom of someone who has learned that the best response to large, uncontrollable things is to do the small controllable thing in front of you, and keep doing it.

He taught the player to throw a Pokéball. Not competitively, not as a game. As a tool of care.

By Phase Three, he is old enough to have slowed down. He does not go into the field anymore. He sits on the back steps in the evening with tea and a quality of patience that the player, running crisis response across all of Hoenn, periodically needs to come home to.

He is the last character the player sees. The epilogue belongs to him.

**Key line:** *"When something gets lost, what do you do? You help it find its way back. Right."*

**Narrative function:** Bookend. He opens the game's emotional world and closes it. In between, he is present but quiet — a resource the player does not need to consult but is glad exists.

### The Player's Parent(s)

**Role:** Bridge generation; civic participants; the living demonstration that the grandparents' values propagated
**Age:** Mid-to-late 40s
**Emotional register:** Practical, loving, slightly tired in the way of people who have been managing a sustained emergency for a decade

The parent(s) are not Rehabilitators by formal designation, but they are deeply embedded in the civic infrastructure of coastal Hoenn — volunteer work, supply chains, community emergency response. They brought the player to Slateport to sandbag as a teenager not because it was required, but because they could not imagine not doing it.

They appear in Phase Two and in Phase Three check-ins. They are not the player's obstacle or the player's savior — they are the player's context. The person who knows what this family does when things get hard.

**Narrative function:** Ground. They make The Holdfast feel like a real lineage, not a setting.

## Maxie

**Role:** Former leader of Team Magma; co-founder of Team Obsidian; the player's ideological antagonist-turned-ally
**Age:** Late 60s
**Emotional register:** Contained, precise, carrying an enormous amount of guilt that he has converted almost entirely into purpose

Maxie was always someone who believed in the rightness of his own analysis. What changed after the original Groudon crisis was not that he stopped believing in analysis — it was that he updated his data. He was wrong. Catastrophically wrong. People were hurt. The ecology was disrupted in ways that are still reverberating.

He has spent the intervening years building Team Obsidian with the same methodical focus he brought to Team Magma, except now directed at repair rather than expansion. He is not redeemed. He is *working*. He would be the first to say those are different things.

He and Archie have an unlikely, genuine partnership that they both refuse to call friendship. They disagree constantly and effectively.

**Key line (reveal scene):** *"We cannot undo what we did. We can refuse to be useless while the bill comes due. That is the most we can offer. I am aware it is not enough. I bring it anyway."*

**Narrative function:** He is the game's argument for *doing the inadequate thing rather than nothing*. He is also the game's caution against letting that argument become a rationalization for forcing outcomes. The player must decide how much weight to give him.

### Character Note for Writers

Maxie should never be played for easy redemption warmth. He is not huggable. He has not softened. He has *clarified*. The right feeling when the player leaves a scene with Maxie is not comfort — it is the slightly uncomfortable sensation of having been engaged honestly by someone who takes you seriously.

## Archie

**Role:** Former leader of Team Aqua; co-founder of Team Obsidian; Maxie's operational partner
**Age:** Late 60s
**Emotional register:** Weathered, direct, still capable of genuine laughter though it comes less easily than it used to

Archie processes differently than Maxie. Where Maxie converted guilt into methodology, Archie converted it into *presence* — he is more in his body now, more attentive to the thing directly in front of him, less concerned with long-term planning and more focused on doing the right thing in the moment he is actually in.

He loved the sea. He still loves the sea. He watched Pacifidlog disappear in the seismic event not from any proximity to it but from a weather station feed, and it hit him in a way he has never fully described to anyone. He has been to the dive site three times. He doesn't know what he's looking for.

He and Maxie work because they are genuinely different people with the same impossible commitment. Archie brings the human element Maxie's plans sometimes lack. Maxie brings the structural rigor Archie's instincts sometimes skip.

**Key line (reveal scene):** *"We're old. We don't have a lot of time to be doing this the slow way. But Maxie keeps telling me the slow way is the one that works, and the one time I didn't listen to him we both ended up here. So."*

**Narrative function:** He humanizes Team Obsidian's cause without softening it. He is the emotional entry point for players who find Maxie too cold — but he carries the same weight and arrives at the same uncomfortable place.

### Character Note for Writers

There should be at least one scene between Archie and the player that happens near water — ocean if possible. He is more himself near water. Let him be.

## Secondary NPCs

### Coordinator Yemi — Slateport Volunteer Operations

**First appearance:** Phase Two, Slateport Harbor
**Role:** The practical face of civic restoration infrastructure

A woman in her 50s who has been coordinating coastal volunteer operations out of Slateport for fifteen years. She knows every regular volunteer by name and has a gift for deploying people to exactly the task that will keep them coming back. She is not warm in a demonstrative way — she expresses care through competence and through the specific attention she pays to whether people are eating and sleeping.

In Phase Three she becomes a logistics ally — her network of supply chains and volunteer contacts overlaps significantly with the EHI restoration infrastructure. She is not affiliated with Team Obsidian and has no interest in becoming affiliated. She is simply someone who has been doing the work longer than most.

**Key line:** *"I don't care what your plan is. I care whether you have enough people and enough food. Answer those two questions and we can talk about the plan."*

### The Boy from the Levee — *Name TBD*

**First appearance:** Phase Two, Slateport sandbag crew
**Role:** The player's peer; the human face of Pacifidlog's loss; possible recurring ally

He is the same age as the player and his cousin's family was in Pacifidlog. He is not angry about this — he is the way teenagers sometimes get when something large has happened: he makes quiet observations that don't quite go anywhere, and then the conversation continues, and the observation just hangs there.

He reappears in Phase Three in a Rehabilitator-adjacent role — he never got the certification but he works informally with the ecological recovery corps. He is the player's closest peer in the narrative: someone who has been shaped by the same history, made different choices about what to do with it, and is genuinely glad the player exists.

**Narrative function:** Grounding. Friendship. The game needs at least one character who is neither mentor nor antagonist but simply *alongside*.

### Nurse Joy — Evolving Role

**Role:** Symbolic and practical transformation of the Pokémon Center system

In Primal Harmony, the Pokémon Centers have adapted their function alongside the broader crisis. Nurse Joy — across all regional Centers — now coordinates Ecological Relocation Terminals: the hub through which players release counter-type Pokémon into Type Overload Zones and return native species to their home biomes.

She has not changed in personality — warm, efficient, attentive — but she has changed in scope. She knows which zones need what. She tracks what has been released where. In certain Centers adjacent to major zones, she has developed specific expertise that makes her a valuable field consultant.

**Key line (Relocation Terminal introduction):** *"The Terminal's pretty new — we installed it six months ago. I won't pretend I understand all of it. But I understand the idea: you put the right thing in the right place, and the place starts to remember what it was. That part makes sense to me."*

## Character Notes — General Principles

**On naming:** Character names should feel like they belong to real people from a coastal, ecologically-aware Pacific Rim-adjacent culture. The Hoenn aesthetic has always carried a light pan-Pacific quality. Names like Yemi, River, Sable, and Archie coexist naturally in this world.

**On age:** The older generation — grandparents, Maxie, Archie — should feel like people at the end of a very long middle chapter, not at the beginning of a redemption arc. They have done their reckoning. What's left is work.

**On the player's interiority:** The player character does not have voiced dialogue. Their emotional state is expressed through player choice and through the environment's response to what they do. Writers should build scenes so that what the NPCs say *around* the player lands differently depending on how the player has played. A high-EHI player hearing Archie's reveal should feel different from a low-EHI player hearing the same words.
